Here is a pattern you will recognize. You ask the model for something, it gives you a perfectly
reasonable answer, and you feel a small flicker of disappointment because it is not what you wanted.
The instinct is to blame the model. The honest read is usually that you knew what you wanted and the
model did not, because the part you cared about most never left your head.

This is the central trap of working with AI. The model cannot hear your intent. It can only read your
words. Everything obvious to you, your project, your reader, the thing you are secretly worried about,
is invisible unless you write it down. The gap between what you meant and what you typed is where bad
answers come from.

## The curse of knowing

You know your situation so well that you forget the model does not. You ask it to "make this more
professional" without saying who reads it or what professional means in your world. You ask it to
"summarize this" without saying for whom or how short. You are not being lazy. You are being human.
The fix is a small discipline of saying the obvious part out loud.

A useful test before you hit send. Read your prompt as if you were a stranger who has never met you.
Could that stranger do the task and get it right? If the answer is no, you have found the gap.

## Three habits that close the gap

- **Name the reader and the goal.** "Help me write an email" leaves out everything. "Help me write a
  short email to a client who missed two payments, firm but not hostile, that ends by proposing a call
  this week" leaves out almost nothing. The second one can only go one way.
- **Say what counts as done.** Tell the model the finish line. "Three options, each under fifty
  words." "A version I could paste into a text message." "Done means I would actually send it." A
  defined finish line is the difference between a draft you can use and a draft you have to redo.
- **Surface the thing you are actually worried about.** This is the move almost nobody makes. If your
  real concern is that the email sounds passive aggressive, say so. "Make sure it does not read as
  passive aggressive." The model is good at hitting a target it can see.

## Vague is a choice, specific is a habit

Specificity feels like extra work in the moment, and it is, by a few seconds. But vagueness is more
expensive. A vague prompt gets a vague answer, which you then have to diagnose, re-explain, and ask
for again. Specific up front is faster end to end, almost every time.

The good news is that you do not have to get it perfect on the first try. You can be specific in
layers. Start with a clear ask, read what comes back, and then correct the one thing that is off.
"Closer, but make it warmer and cut the apology." Each correction is you saying a little more of what
you meant. That back and forth is not a failure of the first prompt. It is how the work is supposed to
feel.

## Try it

Take a request that is too vague to land and make the reader, the goal, and the finish line
impossible to miss.

::practice::

The skill here is not flowery writing. It is honesty about what you actually want, written down where
the model can see it. Say what you mean, and most of the disappointment disappears.
