Skills you do not use evaporate. So this chapter is about putting the habits to work in the parts of
life where you actually live. Pick the one closest to you, try the prompt, and feel it work. The point
is not to read about AI being useful. It is to have a useful moment today.

Every example below uses the moves from earlier chapters, a role, a reader, a shape, a constraint.
Notice the pattern and you can build your own.

## Writing

The blank page is where AI earns its keep. Use it for the first draft, the awkward email, the thing
you have been putting off.

- "You are a clear, friendly writer. Help me write a short message declining this invitation without
  sounding cold. Here is the invitation: [paste it]. Keep it under five sentences."
- "Here are my rough notes from the meeting. Turn them into a tidy summary with action items and who
  owns each one: [paste notes]."
- "Rewrite this paragraph three ways, one formal, one warm, one very short, so I can pick."

## Business and work

Most of the daily grind is reading, writing, and sorting. That is the model's home turf.

- "You are a careful analyst. Here is a customer email. Summarize the complaint in one line, then draft
  a calm reply that offers a next step: [paste]."
- "Give me five names for a coffee subscription aimed at busy parents, with a one-line reason for each.
  Avoid anything cutesy."
- "Here is a spreadsheet of last quarter's sales as text. Tell me the three things that stand out and
  one question I should be asking: [paste]."

## Cooking

A practical kitchen helper that works with what you have.

- "I have chicken thighs, a can of coconut milk, rice, and spinach. Give me one dinner I can make in
  under 30 minutes, with steps. No special equipment."
- "Scale this recipe from four servings to ten and give me a shopping list grouped by aisle: [paste]."
- "Suggest three weeknight dinners for a household where one person does not eat dairy. Keep them
  simple."

## Parenting

A patient sounding board, not a replacement for your judgment, which stays yours.

- "Explain why the sky is blue to a curious six year old, in three short sentences, then give me one
  follow up question they will probably ask."
- "Help me think through how to talk to my ten year old about screen time. I want firm but not a
  lecture. What are a few approaches?"
- "Turn this week's spelling words into a fun ten-minute game we can play at the dinner table: [list]."

## Learning

This might be the most life-changing use of all, a tutor with infinite patience.

- "Explain compound interest to me like I am smart but have never studied finance. Use one real
  example with numbers, then quiz me with two questions."
- "I am learning to play guitar. Build me a simple 15-minute daily practice plan for a total beginner,
  and tell me what to focus on first."
- "I read this article and did not fully follow it. Explain the main argument in plain language, then
  tell me the one part most people misunderstand: [paste]."

## Make it a habit

The people who get the most from AI are not the ones who know the most about it. They are the ones who
reach for it more often, on small things, until it becomes reflex. Next time you catch yourself
dreading a task, drafting, summarizing, deciding between options, explaining something to yourself,
stop and write the prompt. That is the whole game. For dozens more real uses across every category,
each with a copy-able starter prompt, head to the Cookbook and find one from your own life.
