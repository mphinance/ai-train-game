There is a difference between an answer that sounds good and an answer you can act on. AI is very good
at sounding good. That is exactly why you need a few habits for getting output you can actually trust,
because a confident wrong answer is more dangerous than an obviously bad one.

Trust does not come from hoping the model is right. It comes from two things you control. First, you
shape the request so a good answer is the easy answer. Second, you verify the part that matters before
you rely on it. Neither takes long, and together they move you from crossing your fingers to checking
your work.

## Shape the request

The shape of your ask largely determines the shape of the answer.

- **Ask for structure.** "Give me marketing ideas" gets you a soft paragraph. "Give me five marketing
  ideas as a numbered list, each with the idea, who it targets, and the rough cost" gets you something
  you can scan, compare, and decide on. Structure forces the model to be complete.
- **Ask for the right size.** A one-line answer and a full plan are different requests. Name the size
  you need so you do not get a thesis when you wanted a sentence, or a sentence when you needed a plan.
- **Ask it to show its work on anything that matters.** "Walk me through how you got that number" or
  "list the assumptions you made" turns a black box into something you can check. When the reasoning is
  visible, you can catch the one step that is wrong.

## Verify the part that matters

You do not need to fact-check everything. You need to fact-check the parts where being wrong would
cost you. The skill is knowing which parts those are.

- **Names, numbers, dates, and quotes.** These are where models are most likely to be confidently
  wrong. If you are going to repeat a statistic or cite a source, confirm it independently. Treat any
  specific fact as a claim to verify, not a fact to trust.
- **Anything you will put your name on.** A draft is yours the moment you send it. Read it as if a
  skeptic wrote it and you have to defend it. If you cannot defend a line, fix it or cut it.
- **Anything irreversible or expensive.** Money, legal wording, medical or safety questions, a public
  post. The higher the stakes, the more you verify, and the more you treat AI as a fast first draft
  rather than the final word.

A simple move that catches a lot. Ask the model to check itself. "Review your answer and flag anything
you are not confident about." It will often find its own weakest points, which tells you exactly where
to look.

## Build the loop

Put it together and you get a small, repeatable loop. Ask in a clear shape. Read the result. Verify
the load-bearing parts. Correct what is off and ask again. Most people stop after the first answer,
which is why most people get mediocre results. The loop is where trust comes from, and it is faster
than the cleanup you would otherwise do later.

## Try it

Take a loose request and rebuild it so the answer comes back in a usable shape, with a built-in way to
check it.

::practice::

Trustworthy output is not something you find. It is something you set up, by asking well and verifying
on purpose. Do that and you can lean on these tools for real work, not just for ideas you have to
double-check anyway.
