Here is the uncomfortable truth. The people who say AI is overhyped and the people who say it is
magic are usually using the exact same tool. The difference is not the model. The difference is how
they talk to it.

Most people are bad at AI for one reason. They treat it like a search engine. They type a few words,
get back something bland and generic, and decide the technology is not ready. The technology is more
than ready. The instructions were thin, and the model did the only thing it could with thin
instructions, which is guess at the average answer for the average person.

You are not the average person. You have a specific situation, a specific reader, a specific bar for
what counts as good. The whole skill of using AI well is getting that specific stuff out of your head
and into the prompt, so the model is working with your reality instead of its best guess at it.

## The 20 percent

You do not need to read the documentation. You do not need to understand transformers or tokens or
temperature. There is a small set of habits that produces most of the gains, and that is what this
guide teaches. Here is the short version, so you know where we are going.

- **Say who and what.** Tell it who it should be and who the answer is for. A careful editor writing
  for your skeptical boss is a different machine than a generic assistant writing for nobody.
- **Give it the real context.** The model cannot see your situation. The sentence you almost did not
  bother to type is usually the one that makes the answer good.
- **Ask for the shape you want.** A list, three options, a single tight paragraph, a draft email. If
  you do not name the shape, you get the default shape, and the default is rarely what you wanted.
- **Set a limit.** Under 150 words. No jargon. Skip the throat-clearing intro. Constraints make the
  output sharper, not smaller.
- **Treat the first answer as a draft.** The first reply is the start of a conversation, not the
  verdict. "Make it warmer." "Cut the second point." "Now do it for a different audience." This is
  where the real quality lives, and almost nobody does it.

That is most of it. Five habits, and you are already ahead of the large majority of people who touch
these tools.

## Why this matters now

This is not a passing trend you can wait out. The gap between people who can direct AI and people who
cannot is becoming the same kind of gap that opened up around email, or spreadsheets, or search. The
people who got fluent early did not become geniuses. They just stopped fighting the tool and started
using it well, and it compounded.

The good news is that the curve is short. Fluency here is a matter of hours, not years. A handful of
moves, practiced a few times until they are automatic, and you cross from frustrated to capable. That
crossing is the entire point of this guide.

So let us be practical about it. The next chapters take those five habits and turn them into reps you
can actually feel working. You will write a weak prompt, watch why it is weak, and rewrite it into a
strong one. Do that a few times and the habit sticks. That is how this goes from something you read to
something you can do.
