Using AI well is partly a prompting skill and partly a judgment skill. The judgment part is knowing
what to hand it in the first place. Point it at the right kind of work and it feels like a superpower.
Point it at the wrong kind and you get the confident nonsense that makes people swear off the whole
thing. The tool did not change. The fit did.

So here is an honest map. Not the marketing version, the real one.

## Where it genuinely shines

- **Transforming text you already have.** Summarizing a long document, rewriting in a different tone,
  turning messy notes into clean prose, translating, extracting the key points from a wall of email.
  When the raw material is in front of it, the model is excellent and rarely wrong, because it is
  working from your text, not its memory.
- **First drafts of almost anything.** A blank page is expensive. AI is a tireless first-draft
  machine. The draft will be generic until you push on it, but generic and present beats perfect and
  imaginary every time.
- **Explaining and tutoring.** Ask it to explain a concept at the level you actually are, ask follow
  up questions, ask for an analogy, ask it to quiz you. As a patient tutor that never sighs, it is one
  of the best uses there is.
- **Structuring and sorting.** Sorting items into categories, routing, tagging, turning a plain
  question into a database query, reading a chart or a receipt into structured data. The unglamorous
  work that used to need a specialist.
- **Thinking out loud with you.** Brainstorming, pressure-testing a plan, playing devil's advocate,
  listing the options you have not considered. It is a strong thinking partner, as long as you stay the
  one who decides.

## Where it fails, and why

- **Confident wrong facts.** A model will state a plausible falsehood with the same calm tone it uses
  for the truth. It does not know what it does not know. This is why the verifying habit from the last
  chapter matters. Treat specific facts, names, numbers, and quotes as claims to check.
- **Anything that needs current, private, or local truth.** Unless you give it the information or
  connect it to a live source, it does not know today's news, your company's numbers, or what is in
  your fridge. Out of the box it answers from training, not from your reality.
- **Real math and precise counting.** It can reason about math, but plain models are unreliable
  arithmetic engines. For anything that has to be exact, have it use a calculator or show its steps so
  you can check, and do not trust a number just because it looks crisp.
- **Genuine judgment with stakes.** Medical, legal, financial, and safety decisions are yours. AI can
  help you understand the landscape and draft the questions. It should not be the one who decides, and
  it will not be accountable when it is wrong. You will.

## The honest takeaway

The pattern is simple once you see it. AI is strongest when the truth is in the room, your text, your
documents, your context, and weakest when it has to supply the truth from memory. Aim it at
transformation, drafting, explaining, and structuring, and verify anything load-bearing. Do that and
the failure modes mostly stop being a problem, because you stopped asking it to do the things it is bad
at.
